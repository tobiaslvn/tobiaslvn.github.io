Notes from Tokyo

March 2026

I've been here four times now, and each visit lands differently. The first time I came for a trade show — nervous, overpacked, jet-lagged into a kind of useful clarity. This time I came with no agenda, which Tokyo doesn't especially reward. The city is built for purpose. Everyone is going somewhere specific.

What I keep noticing is the relationship between restraint and care. A bowl of ramen that took thirty years to perfect, served without ceremony. Packaging designed as though the contents are sacred. The guy at the konbini who bags your items in a particular order, not out of habit but out of something closer to respect. I don't know what to call it in English. The Japanese have several words for it.

I spent an afternoon in Yanaka, which is the part of Tokyo that survived the bombing and the earthquakes — old wooden shopfronts, cats on walls, a cemetery that doubles as a park. A woman was sweeping the path outside her house with a broom made of bound twigs. She had been doing it long enough that the motion looked like breathing.

There's a version of this city that people visit to feel ahead of something — the tech, the fashion, the food. I understand the appeal. But what stays with me is the opposite feeling. A sense that certain things have already been figured out, and quietly maintained, while elsewhere we keep proposing new frameworks for the same problems.

I bought a notebook and a bottle of ink. I didn't write anything in it until I got home.
