On leaving things unfinished

April 2026

I have a folder on my desktop called "ongoing." It contains forty-three items. Some are projects, some are notes, some are single images with no context that I clearly found important at the time. Most of them are unfinished. A few are probably finished and I haven't admitted it yet.

There's a distinction I've been trying to hold onto between things that are unfinished and things that are abandoned. Abandonment is a decision, even when it doesn't feel like one. Unfinished means the thread is still live — you just haven't pulled it recently. The problem is that after enough time, it's hard to tell the difference from the outside. And from the inside, there's always a reason it was worth keeping.

I used to think finishing things was about discipline. Push through, close the loop, ship it. That's mostly true. But I've also watched myself finish things at the wrong moment — complete them before they were ready to be completed — and the result is a kind of hollowness that's worse than leaving them open. A finished thing that wasn't done is harder to return to than one you never closed.

The things I'm most glad I left unfinished are the ones I came back to with different eyes. A project I set aside at twenty-six because I didn't have the vocabulary for it yet. A piece of writing that needed me to go and live something before I could say anything true. Time isn't always delay. Sometimes it's part of the process and you don't get to know that in advance.

None of this is an argument for not finishing things. Most of those forty-three items should probably be deleted. But I think there's a version of completion that includes sitting with something open long enough to understand what it actually wants to be — and that version requires leaving it unfinished for a while.

I still haven't cleaned out that folder.
